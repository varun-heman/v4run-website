title: The Collapse of the Indus Valley Civilisation and What It Means for State-Building
url: https://aeon.co
source: Aeon
date: 2026-05-20
tags: history, governance
thumb: https://picsum.photos/seed/indus/400/300
description: A fascinating re-examination of why the Indus civilisation didn't collapse so much as transform — and what that tells us about how complex societies actually change. The argument that decentralisation was a feature, not a failure, has stuck with me.

---

title: How Courts Became Data Deserts
url: https://thewire.in
source: The Wire
date: 2026-04-10
tags: judiciary, open data
thumb: https://picsum.photos/seed/courts/400/300
description: An uncomfortable look at how India's judicial system produces enormous amounts of information that effectively disappears. The gap between what courts record and what anyone can actually use is wider than I'd assumed before working in this space.

---

title: The Aesthetics of Infrastructure
url: https://placesjournal.org
source: Places Journal
date: 2025-11-03
tags: design, systems
thumb: https://picsum.photos/seed/infra/400/300
description: Infrastructure is usually invisible until it fails. This piece argues that the invisibility is a design choice — and that making infrastructure legible might be one of the most important design challenges of our time. A lovely read.

---

title: What Complexity Science Says About the Future of Cities
url: https://aeon.co
source: Aeon
date: 2026-02-14
tags: systems, governance, design
thumb: https://picsum.photos/seed/cities/400/300
description: Cities aren't planned so much as grown. This is a dense but rewarding exploration of why top-down urban planning consistently fails and what emergence-based thinking could offer instead. Some of the best thinking I've read on why systems resist control.

---

title: The Quiet Erosion of Judicial Independence in South Asia
url: https://thediplomat.com
source: The Diplomat
date: 2026-01-28
tags: judiciary, governance
thumb: https://picsum.photos/seed/judicial/400/300
description: A careful comparative look at how courts in India, Pakistan, and Bangladesh have been incrementally constrained — not through dramatic coups but through appointment processes, funding decisions, and procedural reforms. Important context for anyone working in this space.

---

title: Seeing Like a State, Fifty Years On
url: https://bostonreview.net
source: Boston Review
date: 2025-09-17
tags: governance, systems, history
thumb: https://picsum.photos/seed/state/400/300
description: A retrospective on James Scott's landmark book, asking what its core ideas about legibility and high modernism look like in a world of algorithmic governance and datafication. Turns out the problems Scott identified have gotten more, not less, relevant.

---

title: AI and the Justice Gap
url: https://harvardlawreview.org
source: Harvard Law Review
date: 2025-08-04
tags: ai, judiciary, open data
thumb: https://picsum.photos/seed/aijustice/400/300
description: A sober and well-argued piece on where AI can and can't help with access to justice. The authors are careful not to oversell, which makes the genuine opportunities they identify more credible. Required reading before anyone builds another legal chatbot.

---

title: The Problem With Evidence-Based Policy
url: https://aeon.co
source: Aeon
date: 2025-06-22
tags: governance, systems
thumb: https://picsum.photos/seed/evidence/400/300
description: Evidence-based policy sounds like an obvious good. This piece complicates that assumption — arguing that the demand for RCT-level evidence systematically disadvantages certain kinds of interventions and certain kinds of contexts. A useful challenge to technocratic assumptions.

---

title: On the Ground Floor of Open Data
url: https://restofworld.org
source: Rest of World
date: 2024-11-30
tags: open data, systems
thumb: https://picsum.photos/seed/opendata/400/300
description: Reporting from three countries on what happens when governments release data but no one comes — and the small teams trying to bridge the gap between policy commitments and actual use. Close to my own experience in more ways than one.

---

title: Why Indian Villages Keep Reinventing Local Democracy
url: https://thewire.in
source: The Wire
date: 2024-09-11
tags: governance, history
thumb: https://picsum.photos/seed/panchayat/400/300
description: A fascinating account of how panchayat institutions have adapted and transformed across decades, often in ways that central planners didn't anticipate and wouldn't have designed. Decentralisation in practice looks nothing like decentralisation in theory.

---

title: The Promise and Limits of Computational Law
url: https://lawreview.uchicago.edu
source: University of Chicago Law Review
date: 2024-07-19
tags: ai, judiciary
thumb: https://picsum.photos/seed/complaw/400/300
description: A long but worthwhile read on the theoretical limits of trying to formalise legal reasoning. The section on how context-dependence in law resists algorithmic capture is the clearest treatment of the problem I've encountered.

---

title: Design After Growth
url: https://placesjournal.org
source: Places Journal
date: 2024-04-05
tags: design, systems
thumb: https://picsum.photos/seed/designgrowth/400/300
description: What does design practice look like when you stop assuming that more resources, more scale, and more technology are the answer? This piece explores design in contexts of scarcity and constraint and finds it surprisingly generative.
